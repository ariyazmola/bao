How do oral bacteria impact our health?

  • Home
  • Blog


Since the dawn of time, our bodies have been home to millions of microorganisms living in harmony bacteria. These remarkable single-celled organisms thrive throughout the human body, and your mouth, with its ideal living conditions, is one of their favorite habitats! But here's the good news: as long as their balance remains undisturbed, there's no need to fear oral bacteria. So, what disrupts this delicate balance (known as your "oral microbiome")? What happens when harmful bacteria take over? And how can you prevent these imbalances? This article explores these questions and more, discover our top tips for maintaining optimal oral health!


  1. The Role of Oral Bacteria
    Brushing your teeth in the morning and before bed is essential. Use a soft-bristled toothbrush and fluoride toothpaste. Take at least two minutes, making sureDid you know experts estimate about 100 million bacteria live in every milliliter of saliva? The good news: with proper oral hygiene, these colonies (containing over 600 species!) are completely harmless. In fact, they help maintain optimal oral and overall health. to clean all surfaces of your teeth gently but thoroughly.

    However, the opposite is equally true! When bad bacteria overwhelm your oral microbiome, the balance is disrupted. This makes your mouth vulnerable to infections while increasing risks for diseases affecting other organs.


  2. Health Impacts of Bacterial Imbalance
    An unbalanced oral flora first attacks mouth structures: some bacteria erode enamel (causing cavities), while others infect gums (causing gingivitis). Untreated gingivitis progresses to periodontitis, damaging teeth's support structures.

    But the damage doesn't stop there. Studies confirm oral bacteria can reach the heart, pancreas, lungs, and even weaken immunity. The lesson? Many diseases originate in the mouth - powerful motivation to improve dental hygiene!


  3. Balance-Disrupting Factors
    Your oral microbiome's equilibrium depends on:

    • Oral hygiene quality
    • Saliva pH levels
    • Oxygen concentration
    • Immune system strength
    • Dental anatomy
    • Genetic factors



  4. Optimal Care Practices

    • Meticulous home care: Brush teeth+tongue twice daily, floss, use interdental brushes
    • Professional cleanings every 6 months to remove unreachable plaque/tartar
    • Healthy diet: Limit sugars/acids, stay hydrated
    • Address risk factors: Stress, mouth breathing



  5. Professional Cleanings' Importance
    Regular cleanings:

    • Oral hygiene quality
    • Saliva pH levels
    • Oxygen concentration
    • Immune system strength
    • Dental anatomy
    • Genetic factors
